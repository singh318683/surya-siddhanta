The Surya Siddhanta is one of the oldest surviving works of Indian astronomy, composed in terse Sanskrit shlokas that were built to be memorized and chanted, not read like a modern textbook. It works entirely from a geocentric model: Earth sits still at the center while the Sun, Moon, and five visible planets — Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn — circle around it. There's no Uranus, Neptune, or Pluto in it, because nobody on Earth had detected them yet.

Historians date the surviving version of the text to somewhere between the 4th and 9th century CE, with later scholars often pointing to a revision around 800 CE. It's known today mainly through a 15th-century palm-leaf manuscript and a long chain of commentaries — at least 26 named ones, plus 8 anonymous, spanning over a thousand years. Almost every commentator rearranged or modified the chapter order somewhat, which is part of why titles and numbering can vary slightly between editions.

It mattered well beyond India, too. The solar half of the Hindu calendar still in use today is built on its methods, and it was one of just two Sanskrit works translated into Arabic during the 8th-century reign of the Abbasid caliph al-Mansur, feeding directly into medieval Islamic astronomy.

Of the 18 original Siddhantas

Thirteen are lost to history entirely. The Surya Siddhanta survived because it was copied, taught, and argued over more than any of the others.

— per D. Pingree's survey of Sanskrit astronomical literature

Chapter 12

Where the cosmology held up — and where it didn't

The text states plainly that Earth is a sphere floating in space with no "above" or "below," which was a genuinely sharp claim for its time. Its physical estimates, measured in an ancient distance unit called the yojana, tell a more mixed story.
